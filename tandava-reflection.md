# TĀṆḌAVA — a guided tour through the end of the world

**Biniza Vázquez | A01737294**
_Written reflection accompanying the visual piece (website): [URL]_

---

Welcome. Before we walk in, I need you to leave one thing at the door: the idea that "the end of the world" is an ending.

I know. That is exactly what we came here to discuss — _are we heading toward paradise or toward hell?_ — and I promise we will answer it. But this piece was built on a suspicion I have been carrying all semester: that the question itself was written in only one alphabet. The Western apocalypse assumes that time is a straight line.. a beginning, a climax, a final judgment. My piece is written in another grammar, the one I have been learning through Hindu philosophy: time as a wheel.

So instead of a painting or a collage on paper, I built a website. Not because I couldn't print something (_I promise_), but because code was the only medium that could **perform** the argument instead of just illustrating it. A printed apocalypse ends at the edge of the page. Mine doesn't end at all. Let me show you.

## the ring

The first thing you see is a circle of small white particles, appearing and disappearing in the dark. Look closer: no particle survives. Each one is born, drifts, and annihilates, and the ring never breaks. I coded it as a quiet homage to two things at once: the _prabhamandala_, the ring of fire that surrounds Shiva Nataraja, and the subatomic dance physicists observe inside particle accelerators, where matter is created and destroyed without pause. Destruction is not the opposite of existence here. It is its rhythm.

## the dance

Shiva Nataraja holds a drum in one hand (the heartbeat that creates the world) and a flame in the other (the fire that dissolves it). This perfect balance is the equilibrium that makes us chaotically singular. There is a two-meter bronze Nataraja standing outside CERN in Geneva, gifted by the government of India in 2004 — the physicists recognized their own equations in his pose. That statue is the thesis of my piece: science and metaphysics describing the same dance in two languages. In this cosmology, the "end of the world" is an oversimplification of _pralaya_: the cyclical dissolution, the exhalation of Brahman before the next inhalation.

## the flash

Then the page inverts... Black becomes white, the way the desert of Alamogordo did at 5:29 a.m. on July 16, 1945, when the Trinity test detonated the first nuclear weapon. This is the only "color change" the piece allows itself, because this is the moment the West finally built its own apocalypse... and the detail that obsesses me is this: J. Robert Oppenheimer, watching his creation, did not reach for the Book of Revelation. He reached for the Bhagavad Gita: _"Now I am become Death, the destroyer of worlds"_ (11.32). The West invented the end of the world and needed the East to name it.

## what fire cannot burn

Here the tour slows down. In the middle of a black-and-white piece, one image keeps its color: a small orange flame, placed under verse 2.23 of the Gita: _weapons do not cut it, fire does not burn it._ The verse speaks of the _atman_, the self that is never born and never destroyed. So I ask the question the piece was built around: if there is something in us that not even a bomb reaches, what does an apocalypse actually destroy? Only forms. Bodies, cities, structures, expectations. People do not fail us; our expectations of them do. And worlds do not end; our ideas of how they _should be_ end. What burns is the veil, and "apocalypse," in Greek, never meant destruction. It meant _revelation_: to lift the veil.

## the arrow and the wheel

Physics does offer its own slow apocalypse: the second law of thermodynamics, entropy always increasing, stars going out, everything dissolving. A straight arrow of time. The piece closes by placing that arrow next to the wheel, and next to a song: _Entropy_ by Daniel Caesar, which borrows the language of physics to talk about watching things come undone and finding something like peace in that certainty. I embedded it as the soundtrack of the piece because it proves my point from inside our own culture: even our pop music suspects that dissolution and tenderness are not enemies.

## pralaya

At the bottom of the page there is a button: **dissolve this world**. Press it. The screen floods with light, everything disappears, and you are returned, gently, to the ring of particles where we began. You cannot reach the end of this website. That is not a glitch. That is the answer.

**So: paradise or hell?** Neither — and I don't say that to avoid the question, but to dissolve it. If time is a wheel, hell is a station, not a destination… and so is paradise. We are not _heading_ anywhere final; we are mid-dance, somewhere in the dark stretch of the cycle, which the texts call Kali Yuga and which we recognize every time we read the news. But the essence — the dancer behind every form — has no beginning and no end. It never arose; it will never disappear. It is not reached. It is recognized.
